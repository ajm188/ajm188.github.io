A few weeks ago someone brought Swiss chocolate into the office to share.
Among the options was a bar of white chocolate.
Looking at the bar, immediately I wondered: *what exactly makes white chocolate white?*
I did some digging, and here's what I found out.

When cocoa beans are harvested and processed, they are split into two components.
The first of these is cocoa butter.

### Cocoa Butter

Cocoa butter is the fatty part of the bean.
It's pale-yellow in color (hint hint) and melts at just above room temperature.
This convenient melting point makes it easy to extract from the bean.
The beans are hung in bags in a warm room, and the fat melts off the beans and is collected.
Cocoa butter can have a strong taste, and so is sometimes deodorized.
The remnant of the beans is then ground into the second component of chocolate.

### Cocoa Solids (Cocoa Powder)

Cocoa solids have a poor definition.
In essence, cocoa solids are the contents of the cocoa bean after extracting cocoa butter.
The cocoa powder you buy in a store is the ground up form of these solids.
An important item to note regarding cocoa solids is its dark color (again, hint hint).

Cocoa solids are rich in the antioxidants that you read about in all the articles touting the health benefits of chocolate.
This explains why darker chocolates are considered better for you.

## Making Chocolate

To make chocolate, first you will need what is called cocoa liquor (the melted form of cocoa mass).
Cocoa mass is a paste made of the cocoa bean.
It contains cocoa solids and butter pretty much in the same proportions that they occur in the bean.
For the curious, the cocoa bean is between 54-58% cocoa butter.
Modern cocoa liquor is around 53% cocoa butter.
I'm still not sure what accounts for this difference.
While I'm still on a tangent, "liquor" has nothing to do with the alcoholic meaning, but literally from the fact that it is a liquid.

Anyway, you start with the beans, make them into a paste, and then melt it into cocoa liquor.
Trying to picture this, all I can think of is the chocolate river from *Charlie and the Chocolate Factory*.

In the past &mdash; like, way in the past &mdash; chocolate was made directly from cocoa liquor.
After it was well-mixed, the liquor was cooled until it became solid again.
The closest thing we have to this today is baking chocolate, which most of us find too bitter to enjoy on its own.

Instead, we now add cocoa butter to the mixture before it is cooled.
This is why it's important to know how to extract cocoa butter from the bean, since our tastes no longer align with its natural composition.
We also add other ingredients to sweeten the liquor before cooling it, like milk and sugar.
It seems to me that we like the idea of chocolate, but just can't bear how bitter it tastes.
I kind of want to try what I'll call Unadulterated Chocolate, just to see what it tastes like.

Since the butter is pale-yellow and the solids are the dark portion, our adding extra cocoa butter means that the chocolate of the past was likely much darker than the chocolate we make today.
In fact, even today's "dark" chocolate has added cocoa butter.

## The Shocking Truth about White Chocolate

So where does white chocolate fit in to all of this?
White chocolate is made **without** cocoa solids.
Instead, it consists of cocoa butter, sugar, and milk, though sometimes vanilla is added for flavoring.

This means that white chocolate is, in fact, *not chocolate*!
It is actually classified as a "chocolate derivative."

Recall that the cocoa solids are what give milk and dark chocolate its delicious-looking brown color.
Since white chocolate has no cocoa solids, it takes on the color of cocoa butter, which is pale-yellow!
Mixed with milk in sugar, one may even see that color as white.
Mystery solved.

Also, since it does not contain cocoa solids, white chocolate lacks the antioxidants that make other chocolates good for you.

## Conclusion

I don't know about you, but I can sleep much easier now that I know why white chocolate has its color.
On the other hand, I toss and turn knowing that we think this mere derivative is actually chocolate!
If you care at all about the integrity of chocolate, you will join me in henceforth referring to white "chocolate" as white chocolate derivative.
